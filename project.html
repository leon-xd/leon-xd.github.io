Crani-Arm

Aug 2021 - May 2022

Overview

The goal of Crani-Arm was to use sEMG sensors to detect finger motion and replicate it in a robotic hand. I was on the software team for this project, where we were responsible for creating the ML models, creating a testing framework, and collecting data. The hardware team was responsible for collecting and processing the sEMG signals, and actuating the servos which controlled the robotic arm. The mechanical team was responsible for designing and fabricating the mechanical hand which would eventually replicate the movements we desired. This was my final Makers project, and was the largest project that I have embarked on.

Testing Procedure

The first thing that we did to demonstrate the power of our EMG sensors was to build a demo that could demonstrate the responsiveness of sEMGs. To do this, we developed a clone of the game "Flappy Bird" using pygame that used signals from the sensors to tell the bird when to jump.

Next, for the collection of data we needed automatic detection of hand position. Using the mediapipe library, we were able to determine hand position through our laptop camera. This helped us to automatically label training data for our machine learning algorithms.

The next thing we had to do was collect data. By synchronizing data from our EMG sensors and camera data, we developed a script to synthesize training datasets.

LSTM Training

By splitting our datasets into training & testing sections, we trained neural networks to recognize and predictively label the specific hand motions. To accomplish this, we used the Tensorflow library for model development, along with scikitlearn, pandas, numpy, and matplotlib. Initially, we attempted to determine a binary "open/close" for each finger, but we quickly realized that our dataset did not have a high enough resolution to predict so many potential combinations. We moved towards recognizing specific, pre-determined hand positions.

For the model, we decided to use a LSTM (Long short-term memory) network, which is a stateful recurrent model that is great for time-series data. During the process of training we spent a long time shifting parameters to determine what the best combination of parameters to predict the data would be.

Robodog

Jan 2021 - May 2021

Overview

Not all projects are destined to succeed, and nothing taught me that more than my work on Robodog. This was my Makers project during the second semester of my Junior year, which was a semester that was still online due to COVID. The project's scope was incredibly large: we wanted to build a functioning robotic dog Ã  la Boston Dynamics that could walk and be controlled via an XBOX controller. A project that is this large, understaffed, and almost completely virtual is naturally doomed to fail, and although we didn't deliver on our original scope, I learned an incredible amount through the process of trying to create the robotic dog.

Software

Through an open source project called Spot Micro, we were able to base our code off of an existing repository. Setting this repository up and thorougly understanding the software behind it allowed me to be exposed to Raspberry Pis, ROS, and the Pybullet simulation library. In addition, hacking into an XBOX controller allowed me to familiarize myself with Bluetooth and Linux. I was able to successfully set up the software onto our robot, when we realized that there were problems with the mechanical assembly that unfortunately involved superglue, so my work could not progress any further.

Electrical

Because I was a team member in Los Angeles at the time that could work on the robot in person, I was also in charge of electrical assembly. This involved figuring out how to hook up a 12V battery to power a servo board with 12 different servos and a Raspberry Pi operating at different voltages. This allowed me to understand voltage regulators, and understand practical knowledge about stable current draw from batteries and the necessity of using proper gauged wires. Although I'm not sure this would be very hard for an electrical engineer, this was a great extension of my robotics knowledge from high school.

Space Painter

Aug 2020 - Nov 2020

Overview

This project's objective was to build a robotic arm with a light attatchment at the end. We programmed it to move so when you took a long-exposure photograph of the arm, it would produce a stunning image. This team worked remotely during the COVID-19 pandemic, and I was on the software team. My responsibilities included writing software to modularize the stepper motor movement, and writing code to produce images.
